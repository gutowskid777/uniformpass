# Persona-Journey Audit — Jen, the DIY Seller (list it herself, keep 100%)

## 1. Who she is

Jen, 41, Oradell, Bergen Catholic parent, FB Marketplace power-seller. In her own words: **"I want to post my 3 items in under two minutes, set my own price, and have a buyer from my own school texting me tonight — with less hassle than Facebook, not more."**

She is not a first-time internet seller who needs hand-holding. She is a skeptical, fast-moving comparison shopper who has one question running in the background of every screen: *why not just post this on Facebook?* The only honest answer UniformPass has is "because the buyers here are guaranteed to be from your kid's school" — every extra tap, field, or wall that doesn't reinforce that answer is a point against the app.

## 2. The ideal journey (designed from scratch)

Each step does exactly one job. Total target: under 2 minutes for item #1, under 45 seconds for items #2 and #3.

**Step 0 — Entry.** She lands on the homepage (from the flyer, a text from a friend, or a Google search). The dominant visual on the page for someone who came to *sell*, not browse, is a single unmissable choice: **"List it yourself, keep 100%"** sitting at the same visual weight as the Auto Sell pitch. Job: tell her in one glance that DIY selling exists and is not the only path (Auto Sell is the other, 50%-cut path — she needs to see both to consciously reject the one she doesn't want).

**Step 1 — Photo, first.** Tap "List it yourself" → camera opens immediately, no form fields yet. This mirrors the FB Marketplace muscle memory exactly (photo before anything else). Job: get the one asset she can't type from memory, before she's asked to think about anything.

**Step 2 — Pick your school, one tap.** Three big buttons for the beachhead schools (St. Joseph Regional / Don Bosco Prep / Bergen Catholic) plus a search fallback. Job: identify the buyer pool. Because the school *is* the town (Montvale/Ramsey/Oradell, NJ — all fixed, known values in the schools table), city and state are never asked again — they're derived silently from the school. She should never see a 51-state dropdown for a 3-school beachhead product.

**Step 3 — One screen: what is it, what size, what condition, what price.** Item description (free text), size (tap chips, not a 30-option `<select>`), condition (3 tap chips), price (numeric, defaults to nothing but is *hard-blocked* from submitting empty — never silently free). Category and gender are inferred from the description where possible and shown as a quiet, already-filled chip she can tap to change — not two more decisions she has to consciously clear. Job: capture only what a buyer needs to decide "yes, contact her."

**Step 4 — Contact, once.** First item only: how buyers reach her (phone/text is the default, prefilled placeholder). Job: make her reachable.

**Step 5 — Post, and the account happens for free.** The same phone number she just typed for "how buyers reach you" doubles as her login handle; a lightweight password field appears inline on the *same* screen she's already looking at (not a modal that pops up after she thought she was done) — because she's mid-flow, adding one password field to a screen she hasn't left yet doesn't feel like a new destination. Job: satisfy the locked accounts-required rule without ever making her feel like she hit a wall.

**Step 6 — "You're live" + immediate repeat.** Confirmation screen shows the listing card exactly as buyers will see it, a **"Share to your school's Facebook group"** button (this is the actual value prop — put the tool in her hand at the moment of highest motivation), and, since she told the app up front (or the app can just ask) she has more to list, a big **"Post your next item"** button that drops her straight back into Step 3 with school, contact, and account already locked in. Job: keep her in a batch-listing loop instead of making her re-discover the entry point three times.

Total conscious decisions for item #1: school (1 tap), description + size + condition + price (1 screen), contact (1 field), password (1 field). Items #2 and #3: description + size + condition + price only. That's the bar FB Marketplace sets, and it's the bar this app has to clear or lose her.

## 3. Where the current build diverges

**City + State are asked as separate, unlinked required fields, even though the school already pins the town and state.**
`app/new/page.tsx:366-381` renders a free-text City input next to a 51-option `US_STATES` `<select>` (`lib/supabase.ts:84-90`), and both are gated in `validate()` at `app/new/page.tsx:140-141`. She picked "Bergen Catholic" two fields earlier via `SchoolPicker`, and the `schools` table row for Bergen Catholic already carries `city`/`state` (`lib/supabase.ts:11-13`) — the app is asking her to re-type information it already has. **Correct behavior:** derive city/state from the selected school and only expose an editable override if she picked "other" school. **Why it matters:** FB Marketplace never asks a seller to hand-pick her own state from an alphabetical 51-item list — it already knows where she is. Re-asking a question the app already answered for itself two fields ago is the exact "this app doesn't know what it's doing" signal that pushes a Facebook-fluent seller to bail and repost there instead.

**The Price field is marked required but is never actually validated — an empty price silently posts as "Free."**
`app/new/page.tsx:359` shows `Price ($) *` with a required-field asterisk, but `validate()` (`app/new/page.tsx:135-144`) has no check on `form.price`. `buildPayload` (`app/new/page.tsx:122`) does `Number(form.price) || 0` — so a blank field becomes `0`, and both the browse card (`components/BrowseExperience.tsx:384`) and the listing detail page (`app/listing/[id]/page.tsx:133`) render `0` as **"Free"** in large bold text. **Correct behavior:** treat price exactly like the other required fields — block submit with a visible error if it's blank. **Why it matters:** Jen is explicitly the persona who "wants full price, not 50%." If she moves fast (as an experienced multi-tab seller does) and tabs past a field she believes the asterisk is already enforcing, her Bergen Catholic blazer goes live advertised as free to the entire school community — and she won't find out until a stranger shows up expecting to take it for nothing. A false-required asterisk is worse than no asterisk: it's a promise the form breaks silently.

**Category and Gender carry required-field asterisks even though both have working defaults and are never actually gated.**
`app/new/page.tsx:245` (`Category *`) and `:255` (`Gender *`) sit next to `uniform`/`unisex` defaults set at form-init (`app/new/page.tsx:32-33`), and `validate()` never checks either. **Correct behavior:** drop the asterisk on fields that already have a sane default and don't block submission — reserve `*` for fields that will actually stop her. **Why it matters:** on a form she's speed-running from muscle memory, every asterisk is read as "I must consciously resolve this or it won't let me through." Two false alarms train her to distrust every asterisk on the page, which is exactly what let the real one (Price) get skipped.

**After posting item #1, she's dropped into a single-listing edit screen with no "list your next item" path — even though this persona has three items to post in one sitting.**
`app/new/page.tsx:194` routes a successful post to `/listing/${listingId}/manage?token=…&new=1`, and that screen's only "you're done" messaging is a static banner — *"Your listing is live! Find it anytime under My Listings"* (`app/listing/[id]/manage/page.tsx:213-219`) — with no CTA to post another item. To list item #2, she has to manually find "+Sell" in the header (`app/layout.tsx:50-55`) or "Sell" in the bottom tab bar (`components/BottomNav.tsx:9`), which reloads `/new` from scratch — her profile prefill carries over name/contact/city/state (`app/new/page.tsx:58-71`) but **not** the school she just picked, so she re-does the school search for items #2 and #3 too. **Correct behavior:** the post-success screen for a fresh listing should default to "Post another item," pre-selecting the same school, and route straight back into the item-detail step, not the edit/manage screen for the item she just finished. **Why it matters:** the app's own success screen treats every listing as a one-off transaction; Jen's real behavior is a three-item batch in one sitting. Every time she has to manually re-find the entry point and re-answer a question she already answered minutes ago, it's another beat where "why not just do all three on FB in one sitting" gets louder.

**The homepage sells Auto Sell (the 50% path) with a loud, dedicated banner; there is no equivalent visible pitch for listing it yourself.**
`components/BrowseExperience.tsx:238-260` renders `ConsignmentBand` — a large emerald gradient block with its own headline, subcopy, and a filled CTA button ("Get a free pickup") that appears on every homepage load, above the listings grid. The DIY path's only homepage presence is the small "+ Sell" pill in the header nav (`app/layout.tsx:50-55`), styled identically in weight to "My Listings" and "Sign in." **Correct behavior:** DIY selling — the path this exact persona will actually convert on, since she explicitly rejects the 50% cut — needs a homepage moment of equal visual weight to Auto Sell, e.g., a two-door module ("We sell it for you, you keep 50%" / "List it yourself, keep 100%") instead of one door getting a banner and the other getting a nav pill. **Why it matters:** a seller who is scanning the homepage for "how do I sell my stuff" sees the 50%-cut option pitched hard and the 100%-keep option not pitched at all — for a comparison-shopping, margin-conscious persona like Jen, that's a design that quietly steers its best-fit user toward the option she'll reject on principle, and toward the wrong first impression that this platform mainly wants a cut.

## 4. The single moment she quits

**The account-creation wall that appears the instant she hits "Post listing," after she believed she was one tap from done.**

She's filled out the entire form — school, item, size, condition, price, city, state, name, contact — the same total effort as a full FB Marketplace listing. She reaches for "Post listing" (`app/new/page.tsx:443-446`) expecting it to behave like every "Post" button she's ever tapped: it posts. Instead, `handleSubmit` (`app/new/page.tsx:201-209`) intercepts with `if (!user) { setShowAccount(true); return }`, and `InlineAccountStep` (`components/InlineAccountStep.tsx`) opens as a modal asking for an email and a 6-character password before anything actually happens.

The copy tries to soften it ("10 seconds, no email to check," `components/InlineAccountStep.tsx:70` / `app/new/page.tsx:459`), and the mechanism is genuinely well-built — deferred signup, nothing re-typed, immediate login, no email round-trip. But the *placement* is the problem: it fires at the exact moment she thought she was finished, not before she started. FB Marketplace has never once asked her to create anything at the point of posting — she already has that account, from years ago, and it's invisible to her now. A brand-new, unexpected "create a password" step landing right on top of her "I'm done, right?" moment is the single highest-probability point where she closes the tab and reposts on Facebook instead — not because the account itself is a big ask, but because it breaks the one promise the "Post" button is supposed to keep.
